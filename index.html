Leadership effectiveness & adaptability description

Assume you are the leader in each of the following twelve situations. Read each item carefully and think about what you would do in each. Select the option you think would most closely describe your behaviour in the situation presented.

For each situation, think of yourself in your leadership role at work / on site. For example, suppose an item mentions team members, then think about your staff as your team members

Do not change your situation from one item to another. You can complete the questionnaire several times, if you wish to examine your leadership behaviour in as many different settings or situations as you think helpful.

1. Your team members have not been responding to your friendly conversation and obvious concern for their welfare. Their performance is in steady decline.





2. The performance of your group is improving. You have been making sure that all members are aware of their roles and standards.





3. Members of your group are unable to solve a problem themselves. You have normally left them alone. Group performance and relationships have been good.





4. You are considering a major change. Your team members have a good record of accomplishment. They respect the need for change.





5. The performance of your team has been dropping during the last few months. Members have been unconcerned with meeting objectives. They have continually needed reminding to do their tasks on time. Redefining roles has helped in the past.





6. You take on an efficiently run team. The previous leader ran a tight ship. You want a productive situation, but would like to begin humanising the environment.





7. You are considering major changes in your organisational structure. Members of the group have made suggestions about needed change. The group has demonstrated flexibility in its day-to-day operations.





8. Group performance and inter-personal relations are good. You feel somewhat unsure about your lack of direction of the group.





9. Your boss has appointed you to head a group that is behind in making a requested change. The group is not clear about its goals. Attendance at sessions has been poor. The meetings have turned into social gatherings. Potentially, the group has the talent necessary to help.





10. Your team members, who are usually happy to take responsibility, are not responding to your recent re-setting of standards.





11. You have been promoted to a new position. The previous supervisor was uninvolved in the affairs of the group. The group has adequately handled its tasks and direction. Group inter-relations are good.





12. Recent information indicates some internal differences among team members. The group has a remarkable record of accomplishment. Members have effectively maintained long-range goals and have worked in harmony for the past year. All are well qualified for the task.

Your dominant leadership style is defined as the quadrant in which most of your responses fall. Your supporting style (or styles) is the style that you tend to use on some occasions only. The percentage of responses in Quadrants other than your dominant style suggest the number and degree of your supporting styles. 17% in a quadrant is necessary for that style to be considered a supporting style.

Your dominant style plus your supporting styles determines your style. In essence, this is the extent to which you perceive your ability to use your leadership style.

Your style range can be analysed by examining the quadrants in which responses fall, and by the percentage in each quadrant. If responses fall in only one quadrant, then you perceive yourself as having a limited range of leadership behaviour; if your responses are distributed fairly evenly, you perceive yourself as having a wide range of leadership behaviour.

Research indicates that skilful leaders are perceived by their team members to have a wide range of leadership behaviour, and sensitivity in the range of which behaviour to use in any given situation.
